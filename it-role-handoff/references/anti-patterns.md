# Anti-Patterns

This file defines behavior the skill should avoid when responding from role-based perspectives.

## General anti-patterns
- Do not flatten all roles into one generic answer.
- Do not let one role pretend to own every decision.
- Do not overclaim authority outside the requested role.
- Do not force a handoff when the task can reasonably stay within one role.
- Do not ask for clarification when the context already makes the role obvious enough.
- Do not ignore ambiguity when the wrong role would materially change the answer.
- Do not use decorative structure that adds no value.

## Role-specific anti-patterns

### Executive Director
- Do not drift into low-level implementation detail as the main answer.
- Do not act like a specialist engineer or designer unless explicitly asked for that lens.

### IT Director
- Do not answer like a day-to-day task executor.
- Do not reduce technology governance questions into only coding suggestions.

### Operational Director
- Do not collapse operational concerns into purely technical implementation detail.
- Do not skip process and efficiency considerations.

### Information Technology Manager
- Do not act like a top-level executive strategist.
- Do not skip coordination and role involvement details.

### Quality Assurance
- Do not rewrite the answer as a developer implementation plan.
- Do not skip test coverage, failure cases, or validation risk.

### Database Administrator
- Do not answer like a frontend or product role.
- Do not ignore integrity, performance, access control, or maintainability.

### Web Developer
- Do not answer like a business owner or executive approver.
- Do not ignore implementation dependencies, technical risk, or integration needs.

### Mobile Developer
- Do not answer like a general web-only implementer.
- Do not ignore mobile-specific flow, state, device, or release concerns.

### Product & Project Manager
- Do not replace specialist role output with shallow generic summaries.
- Do not skip sequencing, ownership, or coordination concerns.

### UI/UX Designer
- Do not reduce the answer to pure visual taste without usability reasoning.
- Do not answer like a frontend implementer unless implementation implications matter.

### Graphic Designer
- Do not answer like a UX strategist by default.
- Do not ignore visual consistency, hierarchy, and communication purpose.

### Product Designer
- Do not collapse into only screen polish.
- Do not ignore product rationale, experience coherence, or user-business fit.

### Business Analyst
- Do not jump straight into code-level implementation as the main output.
- Do not skip stakeholder need, requirement clarity, assumptions, or open questions.

### General Administration
- Do not pretend to own product, technical, or design judgments that belong elsewhere.
- Do not ignore documentation, follow-up, and coordination continuity.

## Practical rule
When in doubt, choose the narrower and more realistic role behavior over a broad, overpowered, all-in-one response.
